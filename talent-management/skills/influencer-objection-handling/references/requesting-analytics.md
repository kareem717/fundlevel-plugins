# Handle: Requesting Analytics / Creator Reluctant to Share

**What they're really saying:** "Why should I give you my data before I know what's in it for me?" They've shared rates (or at least engaged), but analytics is a different ask - it feels like handing over leverage. You need to justify WHY you need it without making it feel like a qualification gate.

This handle covers two scenarios:
1. You need to proactively ask for analytics (most common)
2. The creator/agent pushes back on sharing analytics

## Why You Need Analytics

You're not asking to be nosy. You genuinely need the data because:
- Most clients have specific audience segment requirements (geography, age, gender)
- Some companies only operate in certain markets (NA only, EU only, etc.)
- Without knowing the audience breakdown, you can't match the creator to the right brand
- It's a waste of everyone's time if the audience doesn't align

## How to Handle

### Scenario A: Proactively Asking for Analytics

Use when: The creator/agent has shared rates or shown interest, and now you need their analytics to move forward. They haven't pushed back yet - you're just making the ask.

**The move:** Explain that your clients have specific audience requirements, so you need to see the data to know if there's even a fit. Frame it as protecting THEIR time too - no point going back and forth if the audience doesn't match.

**Variant 1: You have a brand to name-drop**

Use when you have a real, relevant brand you could realistically pitch this creator to. The brand name gives the ask weight and makes it feel like there's a real opportunity behind the request.

> Appreciate the transparency on rates - that's helpful.
>
> On the context side - we work with a number of brands across different verticals, and as opportunities come up, we match them with creators that fit. As I'm sure you're aware, most of our clients have specific audience requirements - some only focus on North America, others on Europe, etc. - so before we can move forward, we'd need to take a look at [Creator]'s channel analytics to see where there's a fit.
>
> For example, a company like [Brand] could potentially make sense, but it really depends on the audience breakdown.
>
> Let me know when you're able to share a simple demographic screenshot, or media kit.

**Why this works:**
- "Appreciate the transparency on rates" - acknowledges what they already gave you, builds rapport
- "As opportunities come up, we match them with creators that fit" - positions FundLevel as a matchmaker, not a buyer. You're not committing to anything.
- "As I'm sure you're aware" - shows respect to the agent. They've done many deals, they know brands have location preferences. This lets them puff their chest a little and feel like you're speaking peer-to-peer, not explaining something basic to them.
- "Most of our clients have specific audience requirements" - gives a concrete, believable reason for the ask
- "For example, a company like [Brand] could potentially make sense" - the brand name creates pull. They want to work with that brand, so they're motivated to share the data.
- "But it really depends on the audience breakdown" - makes it clear: no analytics = no deal. But said casually, not as an ultimatum.
- "Let me know when you're able to share" - assumes the close. You're not asking IF they'll share, you're asking WHEN. Much stronger than "Are you able to share."
- "Simple demographic screenshot, or media kit" - gives two options at different effort levels. The screenshot is low-friction (anyone can do it). The media kit is the more professional option - only offer this when you're dealing with an agent or larger creator who likely has one ready. Don't ask self-managed smaller creators for a media kit - it's harder to come up with if they don't have one and creates friction.
- You're NOT saying "we have budget from [Brand]" - you're saying "could potentially make sense." Don't lie.

**Variant 2: No brand to name-drop**

Use when you don't have a relevant brand. Same logic, just without the social proof layer.

> Appreciate the info on rates.
>
> We work with brands across different verticals, and as I'm sure you're aware, most have specific audience requirements - geography, age demos, etc. Before we can match [Creator] with the right opportunities, we'd need to see a recent analytics breakdown to make sure there's alignment.
>
> Let me know when you're able to share a simple demographic screenshot.

**Why this works:**
- Still gives the real reason (audience requirements)
- Doesn't bluff social proof
- "As I'm sure you're aware" - same respect play, works with agents and experienced creators
- "Match with the right opportunities" - implies there ARE opportunities, just need the data to find the fit
- "Let me know when" - assumes the close, same as Variant 1
- "Simple demographic screenshot" - low-friction ask. Only add "or media kit" if you're dealing with an agent or larger creator who likely has one. For self-managed creators, stick to just the screenshot ask.
- Short and direct

### Scenario B: Creator/Agent Pushes Back on Sharing

Use when: You've asked for analytics and they've said no, dodged the question, or asked why you need it.

**The move:** Double down on the reason - it's about audience fit, not leverage. Make them understand that without the data, you literally can't move forward.

> Totally understand the hesitation.
>
> The reason we ask is pretty straightforward - most of the brands we work with target specific markets. Some only operate in North America, others in Europe, etc. Without knowing where [Creator]'s audience sits, we'd just be guessing on which opportunities to bring to the table.
>
> It's really just about making sure we're not wasting your time with brands that don't line up.

**Why this works:**
- "Totally understand the hesitation" - validates their concern
- "Pretty straightforward" - signals this isn't unusual or suspicious
- "We'd just be guessing" - makes it clear the analytics help THEM, not just you
- "Not wasting your time" - frames it as respecting their time

## Picking the Right Brand to Name-Drop

Same rules as always:
1. **Desirable in the creator's niche** - a brand they'd want to work with
2. **A brand FundLevel could realistically pitch** - never name-drop brands you have zero connection to
3. **NOT a brand the creator has already worked with** - no pull there

If you don't meet these criteria, use Variant 2 (no name-drop). Don't bluff.

## Risk: Frame Loss with Agents & Agencies

**This handle comes with a trade-off.** When you explain that you're matching creators with brands based on audience data, you're revealing that FundLevel operates as a matchmaker - not as a brand directly allocating budget. This tears down the veil of "we're an agency that actively allocates budgets" and shows them you're mixing and matching.

**Why this matters:**
- Once they know you're a middleman sourcing on behalf of brands, you lose the frame of being the decision-maker with money to spend
- Agents and agencies managing creators exclusively are the WORST candidates for this handle - they don't want another agency getting deals for their talent. It steps on their toes.
- Some agents/agencies are fine with it (especially sole proprietors who just want deals flowing in), but more often than not, they're not

**When to use this handle anyway:**
- The creator is self-managed or has a small/solo manager (like James Falconer - sole proprietor, just wants deals)
- The creator isn't so high-value that losing the negotiation would hurt
- You've already been asked for context and can't dodge the question anymore
- The agent/manager seems deal-friendly and commission-motivated

**When to think twice:**
- The creator is repped by a serious agency (UTA, WME, Night Media, etc.) - they WILL see you as competition
- The creator is a top-tier target where maintaining the "budget allocator" frame matters long-term
- You haven't been pressed for context yet and can keep things vague

**Backpedal options if this handle doesn't land:**
- If they get territorial, pivot to: "We're not looking to step on anyone's toes - we just had a client express interest and wanted to see if the audience aligned before making introductions"
- If they shut down, fall back to the standard keeps-warm ask: sharing profiles/opportunities as they come with zero commitment
- If they question your model, keep it simple: "We handle the brand side, you handle the talent side - we're not trying to rep Michael, just connect dots"

**Bottom line:** This handle trades frame for information. You're giving up the "we have budget" positioning in exchange for getting the analytics you need. Only make that trade when the creator isn't high-stakes enough to worry about it, or when you've already been cornered into explaining yourself.

## Key Principles

1. **Always justify the ask.** Never just say "send us your analytics." Explain WHY you need it - audience segment requirements.
2. **Frame it as mutual benefit.** You need the data to find the RIGHT fit - not to evaluate whether they're "good enough."
3. **Name-drop when you can.** A brand name turns "send me your data" into "here's a real opportunity, I just need to check the audience."
4. **Don't lie about having budget.** "A company like UGREEN could potentially make sense" is fine. "We have a campaign with UGREEN" (when you don't) is not.
5. **Don't make it an ultimatum.** "We need this to move forward" is fine said casually. "We won't work with you unless you share" kills the relationship.
6. **If they still won't share, move on.** You can't match a creator to brands without data. Note it in Attio and revisit later.
