# Influencer Objection Handling

Draft replies to creators/influencers who push back on sponsorship pitches. The dynamic here is different from brand objections - you're offering creators paid work, not selling them a service.

## Context

FundLevel reaches out to influencers on behalf of brand clients. The typical first touch is casual and open-ended - no specific rate upfront, just expressing interest in working together on an upcoming video. Rates get discussed later once there's mutual interest.

## Core Philosophy

Every objection is a smokescreen until you find the real reason. A creator who says "not looking for sponsorships" could mean a dozen different things - bandwidth, bad past experiences, rates, creative control, or just laziness in replying. Your job is to diagnose the real reason, not accept the surface-level no.

**The golden rule: ask questions you already know the answer to.**

When a creator says no, you respond with a question designed to keep the conversation going - but you stack the deck so the obvious answer works in your favor. For example, asking "are you trying to stay working with folks like PostHog going forward?" when you know the answer is obviously yes. This does three things:

1. **Social proof** - name-dropping a brand they'd want to work with (PostHog, etc.) signals you have real connections
2. **Fishes out the real objection** - if they say "obviously I want to work with brands like that, but I don't like working with agencies" - now you know the actual problem
3. **Keeps the conversation alive** - they almost have to respond because the question is easy and the answer is obvious

Before drafting a reply, check Attio for the creator's sponsorship history. If they've done sponsorships before, use "going forward" to signal you know this - not "would you ever consider sponsorships."

## When Drafting a Reply

1. Identify which objection type matches (see `references/playbook.md`)
2. Check Attio for the creator's history - past sponsorships, brands they've worked with, etc.
3. Match the creator's energy - casual if casual, formal if formal
4. Keep it SHORT. 2-3 sentences max. Every unnecessary line is a reason to not reply.
5. Ask a diagnostic question that you somewhat know the answer to
6. Name-drop strategically - use brands that would be attractive to THIS creator in THIS niche
7. Cut anything that doesn't serve you. If a sentence provides no value and makes the email longer, delete it.

## Key Differences from Brand Objections

|                       | Brand Objections                                  | Influencer Objections                            |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| **You're selling**    | FundLevel's service (sourcing, production, scale) | Paid work (content they already make)            |
| **Reply length**      | 3-5 sentences                                     | 2-3 sentences                                    |
| **Tone**              | Professional-casual                               | Casual-friendly                                  |
| **Close**             | "Cool if I shoot over a profile?"                 | A diagnostic question with built-in social proof |
| **Name-dropping**     | Drop brand clients (PostHog, TigerData)           | Drop brands the creator would want to work with  |
| **Urgency**           | Low - plant seeds                                 | Zero - creators hate pressure                    |
| **Goal of the reply** | Keep the door open + propose next step            | Diagnose the real reason + keep convo going      |

## Universal Rules

- Shorter is always better. Creators skim. Every extra sentence is a reason to not reply.
- Never mention rates in an objection reply. That conversation comes later.
- Never accept a surface-level no. Always dig for the real reason.
- Ask questions designed so the obvious answer works in your favor.
- Name-drop brands that are cool/desirable in the creator's specific niche.
- "Going forward" signals you know they've done sponsorships before. Use it.
- If a creator has a manager/agent, pivot to them. Don't go around representation.
- Cut ruthlessly. If a line provides no value for you AND makes the email longer, it shouldn't be there.
- Never say anything that implies you'll spam them. No "just helps me know whether to reach out or leave you be" - that plants the idea of being spammed.
