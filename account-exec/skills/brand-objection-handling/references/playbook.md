# FundLevel Objection Handling Playbook

## How to Use This

Every objection is a smokescreen until proven otherwise. The goal is never to "overcome" — it's to keep the door open and stay top of mind. We're not desperate. We're passing along value.

---

## Objection 1: "What do you do / What's your relationship with these creators?"

**What they're really saying:** I don't understand your value prop yet, and I'm not going to take a call until I do.

**How to handle:**
- Give a brief, confident answer — don't over-explain
- Frame it at a high level: "We manage some creators exclusively, have deals with others, and for specific clients we do dedicated outreach"
- Pivot to a call by framing it as *their* benefit: "How we'd structure things depends on what [Company]'s goals are, and that's easier to nail down in a conversation"
- If possible, drop a name (e.g., "One example on the sheet is bigboxSWE, who we're currently running with PostHog")

**Example response:**
> Sure — it's better to hash things out on a quick sync. I grabbed some time on your cal. Quick context in the meantime: we work with ~60 creators — some exclusively, others through deals we have in place. For certain brands we also handle dedicated outreach on their behalf. Mostly I just want to understand what [Company] is after on the creator side and whether there's even a fit.

**Key principle:** Answer just enough to be credible, then redirect to the call where the real value lives.

---

## Objection 2: "These creators don't fit our audience"

**What they're really saying:** You sent me the wrong profiles and I'm questioning whether you understand my business.

**How to handle:**
- Acknowledge immediately — don't defend the list
- Show you understand the mismatch: "Got it — that totally makes sense"
- Reframe: you have a broader network, the initial sheet was just a starting point
- Pivot to a call to understand their needs: "Rather than send over another sheet that might not land right, it'd be quicker to hop on a 10 min call so I can get a better sense of where you're at internally"
- Make the call about pulling the *right* profiles, not pitching

**Example response:**
> Got it — that totally makes sense. We do have creators that skew more toward [their audience], but rather than send over another sheet that might not land right, it'd be quicker to hop on a 10 min call so I can get a better sense of where you guys are at internally and pull the right profiles from there.

**Key principle:** The mismatch is an opportunity to start a conversation, not the end of one.

---

## Objection 3: "We're all set / not interested right now"

**What they're really saying:** I don't see a reason to engage, or I'm busy and this isn't a priority.

**How to handle:**
- Don't push back or try to change their mind
- Respect their position — "No worries at all"
- Shift to a low-commitment ask: permission to send profiles as they come
- Frame it casually: "Cool if I shoot over a profile here and there when I see a fit?"

**Example response:**
> Totally understand! Cool if I shoot over a profile here and there when I see someone that'd be a fit?

**Key principle:** If you can't close, keep the door propped open. A "no" today isn't a "no" forever.

---

## Objection 4: "Budget is allocated / timing isn't right"

**What they're really saying:** We literally can't spend money right now, but we might later.

**How to handle:**
- Don't try to create urgency — the budget constraint is real
- Acknowledge the timeline: "Makes sense, appreciate the transparency"
- Ask about the next planning cycle: "When does FY27 kick off?"
- Offer to stay in touch with a concrete cadence: "Happy to circle back in [month] with some fresh options"
- Or, offer the casual profile pass: "Cool if I shoot over a profile here and there in the meantime? That way you'd have options lined up when planning starts"

**Example response:**
> Makes total sense. Happy to circle back when FY27 planning kicks off. In the meantime, cool if I shoot over a profile here and there? That way you'd have options lined up when the time comes.

**Key principle:** Align yourself with their timeline, don't fight it. Plant seeds for the next cycle.

## Objection 5: "We've already hired an agency"

**What they're really saying:** We have a solution in place and don't see why we need another vendor.

**How to handle:**
- Reaffirm: "Funnily enough, some of our most active partnerships are with brands that already have an agency"
- Position as complementary, not competitive: "We tend to sit alongside them since we're sourcing from a different pool of creators"
- Make it clear you're not trying to replace anyone
- Casual ask: "Cool if I shoot over a profile here and there when I see a fit?"

**Example response (they use an agency, you're offering something complementary):**
> No worries at all, that makes total sense. Funnily enough, some of our most active partnerships are with brands that already have an agency running things. We tend to sit alongside them pretty naturally since we're sourcing from a different pool of creators they might not have on their radar. Either way — cool if I shoot over a profile here and there when I see someone that'd be a fit?

**Example response (they think YOU'RE the agency trying to partner — e.g., Paperlike):**
> Totally get it - just to clarify though, I'm not reaching out as an agency looking to partner. We manage a roster of creators directly - handle everything from matchmaking to production and delivery - and I'm reaching out on their behalf because I think a few of them could be a genuine fit for [Company]. Most of the brands we work with already have agencies running their broader strategy - we just slot in on the creator side with talent they wouldn't typically find through those channels. Cool if I shoot over a profile here and there when I see someone that'd be a fit?

**Important distinction:** FundLevel IS an agency. But you're not reaching out AS an agency looking to partner. The framing is "I'm not pitching you an agency relationship" — not "we're not an agency." If a first clarification doesn't land (e.g., prospect repeats the same objection months later), be more direct and concrete about what you actually do: manage creators, handle matchmaking, production, and delivery.

**Key principle:** "Our best clients already have agencies" reframes the objection into a qualification signal.

## Objection 6: Anchoring to a Low Budget / "Send me someone under $X"

**What they're really saying:** Could be one of several things — and that's exactly the problem. The number itself is a smokescreen. You don't know if it's a hard cap, a test, or just what they think the market costs.

**What to diagnose (the real question behind the number):**
- Is that their total influencer budget? ("We only have $1.5K to spend total")
- Are they specifically looking for smaller/micro creators?
- Are they actually optimizing for CPM, and would be open to a higher upfront if the CPM is the same or better?

**How to handle:**
- Don't just send the cheapest option — that validates the low anchor and you lose leverage
- Ask what's driving the number. Frame it as helping them get the best result, not upselling
- Introduce the CPM angle: a creator at $3K with 2x the reach might be a better deal than someone at $1.5K
- Pivot to a call if the budget conversation gets multi-layered: "This is actually easier to figure out over a quick call"

**Example response:**
> For sure — before I send someone over though, quick question: is the $1.5K a hard cap on what you're looking to spend per creator, or is it more about hitting a certain CPM? Asking because I have a couple options at that range, but also one slightly above that consistently delivers a lower cost-per-view. Want to make sure I'm sending the right fit, not just the cheapest one.

**Key principle:** Don't fulfill a low-budget request at face value. Diagnose what's behind the number — the answer determines whether you send a micro creator, pitch a better ROI option, or pivot to a call.

## Objection 7: "Send proof of work / case studies before we'll talk" (Vetting Gate)

**What they're really saying:** They think you're pitching an agency retainer and want to vet you before investing time. They're treating the call as a sales meeting, not what it actually is — a quick sync to avoid email ping-pong.

**The real problem:** There's a misunderstanding about what you're offering. They think you're trying to close them on an exclusive, brand-allocation basis. You're not. You're trying to understand their goals so you can send relevant profiles on an ad hoc basis. You don't charge for shortlists.

**How to handle:**
- Clarify the misunderstanding immediately — "this isn't an agency pitch, there's no retainer or commitment"
- Explain why you wanted a call: to understand their goals (awareness vs. conversions, dedicated vs. integrated, etc.) so you don't waste their time with a generic list
- If they prefer async, just ask the qualifying questions over email — less effective but still moves things forward
- Name-drop casually to establish credibility without a formal case study deck: "We're currently running creators with PostHog and TigerData on a similar ad hoc basis"
- Don't get defensive about not having a polished website or case study deck — just redirect to the value you're providing

**Example response:**
> Just to clarify — this isn't an agency pitch or anything like that. We don't charge for shortlists and there's no retainer involved. The call was just to avoid a bunch of back-and-forth over email to understand what you guys are going after with creators so I can send the right profiles. But happy to do it async — a few quick questions that'd help me curate something useful: [qualifying questions]. For reference, we're currently running creators with PostHog and TigerData on a similar ad hoc basis.

**Key principle:** When they're vetting you like an agency, the best move is to show them you're not one. Reframe from "prove yourself" to "let me understand what you need." The proof is in the shortlist itself.

## Objection 8: "We already work with these creators directly" (Middleman Objection)

**What they're really saying:** I can get these creators myself - why do I need you in the middle?

**The 3-step handle:**

**Step 1 - Acknowledge & Affirm:**
> "Totally hear ya - and honestly, I already knew you guys were working with a few of these folks ([Creator] included)."

Show you weren't ignorant - you knew about their existing relationships and sent the list intentionally. This flips the frame from "you wasted my time" to "I did my homework."

**Step 2 - Relate to Successful Clients:**
> "Most of our best partnerships are actually with brands that already work with creators directly."

Same reframe as the agency objection - the fact they already do this is a qualification signal, not a disqualifier.

**Step 3 - Actual Handle (the value-add):**
> "We run volume across multiple clients, so we're able to bundle orders and save brands $5K+ on rates. Plus we handle everything from briefing to delivery at scale."

This is where you show what going through FundLevel gets them that going direct doesn't: wholesale pricing through bundled orders, production management, and access to creators at price points they can't get on their own.

**Concrete levers to pull:**
- **Wholesale pricing:** "[Creator] is usually quoted around $60K. Because we bundle across clients, we can save $5K+ on a rate like that."
- **Low-cost entry point:** "Someone like bigboxSWE (who we're running with PostHog) can do a post-roll for as low as $1K."
- **Scale & ops:** "We handle briefing, contracts, revisions, and delivery - so you're not managing multiple relationships yourself."

**Example response (e.g., Railway):**
> Totally hear ya - and honestly, I already knew you guys were working with a few of these folks (Fireship included). The goal wasn't to resurface what you already had, it was to show we're plugged into the same ecosystem and can add value on top. For example - Fireship is usually quoted around $60K. Because we run volume across multiple clients, we're able to bundle orders and save brands $5K+ on rates like that. Most of our best partnerships are actually with brands that already work with creators directly - they just use us to get better pricing and handle the production at scale. On the cheaper end - someone like bigboxSWE (who we're currently running with PostHog) can do a post-roll for as low as $1K. Either way - cool if I shoot over a profile here and there when I see someone that'd be a fit? Even just as a benchmark on pricing.

**Key principle:** If they already work with creators directly, your value isn't access - it's pricing, scale, and ops. Lead with the money you can save them.

## Objection 9: "We'll reach out if anything changes" (Generic Brush-Off)

**What they're really saying:** I'm being polite. I probably won't reach out.

**How to handle:**
- Don't take it at face value — they're not going to remember you in 3 months
- Don't be passive. Take control of the follow-up cadence yourself
- Offer a specific, low-friction value exchange: profiles as they come
- Set your own reminder to re-engage at a natural trigger point (new fiscal year, product launch, etc.)

**Example response:**
> Appreciate that! Rather than leave it open-ended — cool if I just shoot over a profile here and there when I see someone that'd be a fit?

**Key principle:** Never leave the follow-up in their court. Create your own excuse to re-engage.

---

## Objection 10: Hard Shutdown After Repeated "No" (Free Value Disarm)

**What they're really saying:** I'm done with this conversation. You've pitched me multiple times and I'm not buying. Stop selling me.

**When to use this:** You've already tried the standard handles - sent profiles, offered calls, stayed casual - and they've shut you down again. This is the last play before you move on. It only works if you genuinely stop selling.

**The move:** Clarify you're not trying to close them. Offer to send profiles with zero strings attached - the creators are free to reach out on their own. You're just passing along value because you're in the space.

**Why this works:**
- It removes every possible objection. There's no pitch, no call, no commitment, no cost.
- It reframes your role from "salesperson trying to close" to "industry peer sharing relevant info."
- The profiles speak for themselves. If a creator reaches out directly and delivers results, the brand will come back to you naturally.
- It keeps the relationship warm without any pressure.

**Example response (Railway/Sarah - shut down after multiple touchpoints):**
> Hey Sarah,
>
> I hear you - and I appreciate you being straight up.
>
> Being in the space, I know finding good creators can be tedious - so when solid profiles come across my desk, I like to share them with folks they could make sense for, and they're free to reach out on their own.
>
> If you guys are ever looking to expand your roster or want help benchmarking rates, I'm around.
>
> Cheers,
> Kareem

**Key principles:**
- This only works if you actually stop selling after sending it. If you follow up with another pitch, you've burned the technique.
- "They're free to reach out on their own" is the key phrase - it removes FundLevel as the middleman objection.
- "Benchmarking rates" is a low-friction value-add that positions you as a resource, not a vendor.
- Set a follow-up task for 2 months out. If they reply positively, great. If not, move on.
- Don't use "no pitch" or "no strings" disclaimers at the top of the message - those are transparent and signal there IS a pitch. Just offer the value directly and let the actions speak.

## Pattern: Discovery After a Soft Objection

This isn't a standalone objection - it's a technique you layer on top of other handles (especially Objection 3, 6, 7, and 8) when the lead's reply opens a window for qualification.

**When to use:** A lead has given a soft objection (not a hard no), and you want to qualify them before pushing for a call. This works best when:
- They seem open but need more info
- They've done creator work before (check Attio)
- A call would be premature - you need to understand their setup first

**The move:** Lead with evidence that you already know their business, then ask qualifying questions. This turns a qualification form into a peer conversation.

**How to structure the questions:**
- Are they open to flat fees vs. performance-based?
- What content style/format do they prefer? (dedicated, integration, post-roll)
- What's their budget range per creator?
- Are they open to larger campaigns or just one-offs?
- Any preference on creator size? (micro, mid, macro)
- Open to other formats? (podcasts, LinkedIn, X/Twitter)

**Example (CodeCrafters/Emily - soft objection, experienced team):**
> Hey Emily,
>
> Appreciate the reply! Before I send anything over - a few quick questions so I'm not wasting your time with irrelevant profiles:
>
> Are you guys generally open to flat-fee sponsorships, or do you lean more performance-based?
>
> And content-wise - are dedicated videos on the table, or more integrations/mentions?
>
> Cheers,
> Kareem

**Key principles:**
- Don't ask all the questions at once. Pick the 2-3 most important ones for this specific lead.
- If you know they've worked with specific creators before (from Attio), name them: "I saw you guys ran something with [Creator] - are you looking for more of that style, or branching out?"
- This approach works better than pushing for a call when the lead is experienced and knows what they want. They can answer in 30 seconds over email.
- Don't pre-load talk tracks. Ask one round of questions, wait for the answer, then adapt your next move based on what they say.

## The 3-Step Handle Framework

Every objection reply should follow this structure:

1. **Acknowledge & Affirm** - "Totally hear ya" / "That makes total sense." Never argue. Show you understand their position.
2. **Relate to Successful Clients** - "Our best clients actually [X]." Reframe the objection as something your best clients have in common, not a dealbreaker.
3. **Actual Handle** - The concrete value-add. This is where you show what FundLevel brings: wholesale pricing, production at scale, access to creators they can't find, managed service, etc.

Then close with a low-friction next step: "Cool if I shoot over a profile here and there?"

---

## Universal Principles

- **Always reaffirm, never argue.** "That makes total sense" is your best friend.
- **Name-drop subtly.** "One example is bigboxSWE, who we're running with PostHog" > "We work with PostHog!"
- **Calls > emails for complex explanations.** If explaining over email would take more than 3 sentences, pivot to a call.
- **Casual asks keep doors open.** "Cool if I shoot over a profile here and there?" costs them nothing and keeps you in the conversation.
- **You're qualifying them too.** "I want to understand if there's even a fit" takes pressure off and makes you the selector, not the seller.
- **Control the follow-up.** Never end with "reach out if anything changes." Always propose a concrete next step, even if it's small.
