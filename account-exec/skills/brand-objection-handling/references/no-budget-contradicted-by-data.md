# Handle: "No Budget" Contradicted by Data

**What they're really saying:** "No" - but Attio tells a different story. They've done sponsorships recently, they have meaningful ARR or funding, and the "no budget" claim doesn't add up. This isn't a hard budget constraint - it's either a brush-off, a prioritization issue, or they just don't see enough value yet to allocate spend.

## When This Applies

All three must be true:
1. The lead said "no budget" or "budget is allocated" or similar
2. Attio shows recent sponsorship activity (within the last 6-12 months)
3. The company has meaningful revenue/funding (high ARR, recent funding round, etc.)

If any of these are missing, use the standard budget handle (Objection 4) instead. Don't challenge a budget claim you can't back up.

## How to Handle

**Step 1: Verify in Attio before replying.**
- Pull their sponsorship records. When was the last one? Who was the creator? What type (integration, dedicated)?
- Check the company record for ARR, funding, headcount - anything that tells you this company has money
- Look at the sponsorship details: was it manually verified? What video was it linked to?

**Step 2: Use what you found to ask a diagnostic question.**
- Don't say "but I saw you sponsored X" - that's confrontational
- Instead, reference the sponsorship naturally and ask whether it was a one-off or part of a formal program
- The goal is to understand: did someone internally push for that sponsorship? Is there a champion? Or was it truly a one-time experiment?

**Step 3: Based on their answer, go down one of two tracks.**

---

## The Diagnostic Question

The core move is asking whether their recent sponsorship was a one-off or part of a program. This tells you everything:

- **"It was a one-off"** - means there's no formal budget line, but someone internally had enough pull to make it happen once. Find that champion.
- **"We have a program"** - means there IS budget, they just don't want to spend it with you yet. Now it's a value conversation, not a budget conversation.
- **"We're not renewing"** - that's real intel. Ask why. Bad results? Wrong creator? Wrong format? This tells you exactly what to pitch differently.

## Example (Lokalise/Lauren - said "no budget" but Attio shows recent Arjan Codes sponsorship + $12M ARR)

**Context:** Lauren replied saying no budget for influencer work. But Attio shows Lokalise sponsored Arjan Codes (Python/dev creator) within the last few months, and the company has ~$12M ARR with $50M+ in funding.

> Hey Lauren,
>
> I can totally get that for you.
>
> I actually noticed you guys recently did a piece with Arjan Codes - did you find that it drove meaningful results on your end?
>
> And was that more of a one-off, or part of a more formal creator program?
>
> Kareem

**Why this works:**
- "I can totally get that for you" - responds to her request (she asked for a creator list) without making a hard commitment on timing. No false promise.
- "I actually noticed" - shows you did your homework without being confrontational about the budget claim
- "Did you find that it drove meaningful results" - does NOT assume it went well. Maybe it flopped. You're asking, not telling.
- "One-off or part of a more formal creator program" - the diagnostic question. Their answer determines your entire next move.
- Short. Four lines. Easy to reply to.

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't say "I'll send that over shortly" - that's a false promise you might not keep
- Don't say "that must have driven great results" - you don't know that. Even 500K views could mean zero conversions.
- Don't pre-load your next pitch. Ask the question, wait for the answer, then adapt.
- Don't stack multiple questions. One diagnostic question per email.

---

## Response Tracks

Based on their answer to the diagnostic question:

### Track A: "It was a one-off" / "We're not renewing"
- They tried it but aren't committed. Find out why.
- Ask what they were hoping to get from it (awareness? signups? demos?)
- If the results were bad, that's your opening - "What if the format was different?" or "What if the creator was more targeted to your ICP?"
- Look for the internal champion - someone pushed for that one-off. They might push again with the right pitch.

### Track B: "We have a program" / "It's ongoing"
- Budget exists. The objection was a brush-off.
- Now it's a value conversation: what can FundLevel offer that their current setup doesn't?
- Lead with wholesale pricing, managed production, or access to creators they can't find on their own
- "Cool if I shoot over a profile here and there when I see a fit?" works well here

### Track C: No reply
- Follow up in 2-3 weeks with just the creator list she asked for
- Keep it transactional - deliver value, don't re-pitch

---

## Key Principles

1. **Always verify before you challenge.** Pull Attio data first. If the sponsorship data is thin or old (12+ months), don't use this handle.
2. **Never assume performance.** "That must have crushed" is a guess. "Did you find meaningful results?" is a question. Always ask.
3. **No false promises.** "I can totally get that for you" (no timeline) is fine. "I'll send it shortly" (makes a commitment) is not. Say what you'll do only when you're ready to do it.
4. **Don't pre-load talk tracks.** Ask one question, wait for the answer. Your next move depends entirely on what they say. If you stack questions or assumptions, you lose the diagnostic value.
5. **Reference, don't confront.** "I noticed you guys did a piece with [Creator]" - not "But you clearly have budget because you just sponsored [Creator]."
6. **Find the champion.** If they did a one-off, someone internally made it happen. That person is your real prospect.
